The tokenization of human life

2026-03-05

When AI mediates an increasing share of our reality

Introduction

When we talk about tokenization, many people think of crypto assets or points systems. In this essay, I use tokenization in a different sense: an increasing share of life is processed through AI in forms models can handle — tokens, numerical representations, and statistical predictions.

This does not mean human life can literally be reduced to machine units. It means that digital traces of life — text, speech, images, clicks, behavior patterns — are transformed into model-readable form, and that mediated layer begins to shape everyday choices, decisions, and power.

So the key question is not only what AI can do. The key question is what changes when AI becomes the default mediator of ordinary life.

What tokenization means here

It helps to separate three levels:

  1. Technical tokenization: language and multimodal models process inputs as numerical units. This is system mechanics, not a moral claim about human value.
  2. Operational tokenization: organizations turn activity into measurable signals: response times, conversion rates, risk scores, priority classes.
  3. Normative tokenization: measures become values: what is measurable starts to look more important than what is hard to measure.

The third level is the critical one. The problem is not that models use tokens. The problem begins when institutions treat people primarily through their token trace.

How this already shows up

Work

Work communication is increasingly summarized, prioritized, and scored automatically. This can improve productivity: less waste, faster coordination, clearer visibility.

But it also creates risk: value at work may get defined mainly by what systems can easily read. Quiet problem-solving, mentoring, and trust-building can become invisible.

Health

Health data can improve decisions for both individuals and care systems. Preventive tracking can reduce illness, improve targeting, and lower costs.

At the same time, heavy data framing can create pressure to “perform” as an ideal patient. Health can become a continuous optimization project where deviations from the norm are treated as risk rather than ordinary human variation.

Social life and reputation

Ratings, recommendations, and visibility algorithms make trust more scalable. That is a real advantage.

Yet reputation tokenization can make social risk sticky. A single bad rating or context-poor signal can carry disproportionate weight when systems treat it as objective evidence.

What benefits tokenization can bring

It is a mistake to frame this only as threat. Well-designed systems can produce real gains:

  • Better accessibility: AI lowers the threshold to write, analyze, learn, and create.
  • Faster coordination: shared situational awareness emerges faster than with purely manual workflows.
  • Better traceability: high-quality logging can make decisions more auditable than pure intuition.
  • New value-sharing models: contributions can be recognized and rewarded more precisely.

These benefits are real, but not automatic. They depend on design choices, rights, and governance.

Where the biggest risks are

1) Metric-reality confusion

When metrics replace the underlying phenomenon, Goodhart’s law kicks in: people optimize the metric, not the goal. Systems can appear successful while real quality declines.

2) Data poverty and data coercion

Data poverty is not just lack of data. It is also lack of agency over how one’s data is interpreted. If access to services, credit, or opportunities depends on token traces, exclusion can become structural.

3) Loss of context

Tokens do not carry meaning by themselves; meaning comes from interpretation. Decisions based only on model-composed signals can erase local, human context.

4) Quiet transfer of autonomy

AI-assisted systems rarely remove human agency in one step. They move it gradually: first recommendations, then defaults, then automation. Convenience hides the shift, which is why governance matters more than interface polish.

Two realistic paths for the 2030s

Path A: Assistive token economy

Tokenization deepens, but institutions build safeguards:

  • the right to inspect and correct inferred profiles
  • a mandatory distinction between model assessment and binding decision
  • transparency about what is measured and why
  • usable service paths without full behavioral data surrender

In this path, AI is an amplifier, not an authority.

Path B: Ranking-by-default

Token traces become default identity. People are treated primarily through profiles, and deviation from the norm model becomes costly. Not because someone intentionally designed a dystopia, but because optimization routines reward tighter predictability.

Efficiency rises, while human bargaining power shrinks.

What to do now

If tokenization is advancing, we need to define terms of use now.

1) Define non-tokenizable zones

Not everything should be converted into scores. Some health, learning, and relational contexts need protection zones where automated scoring is prohibited or tightly limited.

2) Make practical explainability mandatory

Not abstract transparency claims, but operational clarity:

  • what data was used?
  • what most affected the outcome?
  • how can the decision be challenged?

3) Preserve a right to alternative process

People need access to human review in cases where automated interpretation harms them.

4) Combine metrics with qualitative judgment

Organizations should avoid one-dimensional score governance. Quantitative indicators perform best when balanced with context and qualitative evaluation.

5) Treat digital literacy as civic literacy

People should learn to read and negotiate tokenized systems: what can be inferred, what cannot, and how to manage their own data traces.

Closing

The tokenization of life does not mean humanity disappears into machine units. It means more and more decisions about people are made through token traces.

That distinction matters.

Technology can improve life — if we hold one principle: the human is not the model input; the human is the purpose of the system.

As AI enters everything, the central question is not “what can it do?” The central question is: who gets the authority to interpret life?
